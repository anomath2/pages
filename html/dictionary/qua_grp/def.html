Definitions Concerning Quantum Groups

Definition of Quantum Groups

Let
  • $I$: a Cartan datum.
  • $d_i := \frac{i \cdot i}{2}$ for each $i \in I$.
  • $(Y, X, \langle , \rangle, \Pi^\vee, \Pi)$: a root datum over $I$.
  • ${}^{\prime} \mathbf{f}$: the pre-Lusztig algebra.
  • $\mathcal{I} \subset {}^{\prime} \mathbf{f}$: the radical of the standard form on ${}^{\prime} \mathbf{f}$.
The quantum group is the unital associative $\mathbb{Q}(q)$-algebra $\mathbf{U}$ with generators \[ \{ E_i, F_i, K_h \mid i \in I, h \in Y \} \]subject to the following relations: For each $h,h' \in Y$, $i,j \in I$, \begin{align*} \begin{split} &K_0 = 1, \\ &K_h K_{h'} = K_{h + h'}, \\ &K_h E_i = q^{\langle h, \alpha_i \rangle} E_i K_h, \\ &K_h F_i = q^{\langle h, -\alpha_i \rangle} F_i K_h, \\ &[E_i, F_j] = \delta_{i,j} \frac{K_i - K_i^{-1}}{q_i - q_i^{-1}}, \\ &f(E_i \mid i \in I) = 0 = f(F_i \mid i \in I) \text{ for all } f(\theta_i \mid i \in I) \in \mathcal{I}, \end{split} \end{align*}where \[ K_i := K_{d_i h_i} \text{ for all } i \in I. \]

Hopf Algebra Structure

The quantum group $\mathbf{U}$ has the following Hopf algebra structure ([Lus10, End of Section 3.3]): \begin{align*} \begin{split} &\Delta(E_i) = E_i \otimes 1 + K_i \otimes E_i, \\ &\Delta(F_i) = 1 \otimes F_i + F_i \otimes K_i^{-1}, \\ &\Delta(K_h) = K_h \otimes K_h, \\ &\epsilon(E_i) = 0, \quad \epsilon(F_i) = 0, \quad \epsilon(K_h) = 1, \\ &S(E_i) = -K_i^{-1} E_i, \quad S(F_i) = -F_i K_i^{-1}, \quad S(K_h) = K_{-h}. \end{split} \end{align*}

Positive and Negative Parts

Let $\mathbf{U}^+$ and $\mathbf{U}^-$ denote the subalgebra of $\mathbf{U}$ generated by \[ \{ E_i \mid i \in I \} \text{ and } \{ F_i \mid i \in I \}, \text{ respectively.} \]They are called the positive part and negative part of $\mathbf{U}$.

Cartan Part

Let $\mathbf{U}^0$ denote the subalgebra of $\mathbf{U}$ generated by \[ \{ K_h \mid h \in Y \}. \]It is called the Cartan part of $\mathbf{U}$.

Divided Powers

The elements of $\mathbf{U}$ of the form \[ E_i^{(n)} := \frac{E_i^n}{[n]_i!}, \quad F_i^{(n)} := \frac{F_i^n}{[n]_i!}, \quad i \in I, n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \]are called the divided powers.

Properties

  • A presentation by generators and relations is known.
  • The algebra $\mathbf{U}$ has a Hopf algebra structure.
  • Let $\mathbf{f}$ denote the Lusztig algebra. By the definitions, there exist unique algebra homomorphisms $(\cdot)^\pm : \mathbf{f} \rightarrow \mathbf{U}$ such that \[ \theta_i^+ = E_i, \quad \theta_i^- = F_i, \text{ for all } i \in I. \] The restrict to algebra isomorphisms \[ (\cdot)^\pm: \mathbf{f} \rightarrow \mathbf{U}^\pm \] ([Lus10, Corollary 3.2.6]).
  • $\mathbf{U}^0$ is isomorphic to the group algebra $\mathbb{Q}(q)[Y]$ ([Lus10, Corollary 3.2.6])
  • The multiplication map \[ \mathbf{U}^- \otimes \mathbf{U}^0 \otimes \mathbf{U}^+ \rightarrow \mathbf{U}; x \otimes y \otimes z \mapsto xyz \] is a linear isomorphism ([Lus10, Corollary 3.2.5]), called the triangular decomposition of $\mathbf{U}$.

Memo

  • Quantum groups came up originally in physics. It soon became a new and very rapidly developing field in mathematics. Extensive connections have already been found between quantum groups and various mathematical areas such as Lie theory, low-dimensional topology, group theory, noncommutative geometry, and so on.
  • The simplest case (quantum analogue of $SL_2$) was defined by Kulish and Reshetikhin [KL81].
  • The general case was defined by Drinfeld [Dr85] and Jimbo [Jim85], [Jim86] by generators and relations.
  • The algebra $\mathbf{U}$ is also called a quantized enveloping algebra.
