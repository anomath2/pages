1.3. Closed Subgroups of $GL(n, \mathbb{R})$

1.3.2 Exponential Map

If $X \in M_n(\mathbb{R})$, then the continuous function \[ \varphi: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow GL(n, \mathbb{R});\ t \mapsto \exp(tX) \]is a group homomorphism. We call this the one-parameter group generated by $X$. It is a fundamental result in Lie theory that all homomorphisms from $\mathbb{R}$ to $GL(n,\mathbb{R})$ are obtained in this way.

Theorem 1.3.5

Let $\varphi: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow GL(n, \mathbb{R})$ be a continuous group homomorphism. Then there exists a unique $X \in M_n(\mathbb{R})$ such that \[ \varphi(t) = \exp(tX) \ \text{ for all } t \in \mathbb{R}. \]

1.3.3 Lie Algebra of a Closed Subgroup of $GL(n, \mathbb{R})$

Let $G$ be a closed subgroup of $GL(n, \mathbb{R})$. We define \[ \operatorname{Lie}(G) := \{ X \in \mathfrak{gl}(n, \mathbb{R}) \mid \exp(tX) \in G \text{ for all } t \in \mathbb{R} \}. \]

Theorem 1.3.8

Let $G$ be a closed subgroup of $GL(n, \mathbb{R})$. Then, $\operatorname{Lie}(G)$ is a Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{gl}(n, \mathbb{R})$.

1.3.4 Lie Algebras of the Classical Groups

To determine the Lie algebras of the classical groups, we fix the following embeddings of $GL(n,\mathbb{F})$ as a closed subgroup of $GL(dn,\mathbb{R})$. Here $\mathbb{F}$ is $\mathbb{C}$ or $\mathbb{H}$ and $d = \dim_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{F}$.
We take $\mathbb{C}^n$ to be $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ and let multiplication by $i$ be given by the matrix $J$. Thus, $GL(n, \mathbb{C})$ becomes a closed subgroup of $GL(2n, \mathbb{R})$.
The case of the quaternionic groups is handled similarly. We take $\mathbb{H}^n$ to be $\mathbb{R}^{4n}$ and use the $4n \times 4n$ matrices \[ J_1 := \begin{pmatrix} J_{2n} & O \\ O & -J_{2n} \end{pmatrix}, \ J_2 := \begin{pmatrix} O & I \\ -I & O \end{pmatrix}, \ J_3 := \begin{pmatrix} O & J \\ J & O \end{pmatrix} \]to give multiplications by $i,j,k$, respectively. Thus, $GL(n, \mathbb{H})$ becomes a closed subgroup of $GL(4n, \mathbb{R})$.
We have
  • $\operatorname{Lie}(GL(n, \mathbb{R})) = \mathfrak{gl}(n, \mathbb{R})$.
  • $\operatorname{Lie}(GL(n, \mathbb{C})) = \mathfrak{gl}(n, \mathbb{C})$.
  • $\operatorname{Lie}(GL(n, \mathbb{H})) = \mathfrak{gl}(n, \mathbb{H})$.
  • $\operatorname{Lie}(SL(n, \mathbb{R})) = \mathfrak{sl}(n, \mathbb{R})$.
  • $\operatorname{Lie}(Sp(n, \mathbb{C})) = \mathfrak{sp}(n, \mathbb{C})$.
  • $\operatorname{Lie}(U(p,q)) = \mathfrak{u}(p,q)$.
  • $\cdots$.

1.3.5. Exponential Coordinates on Closed Subgroups

By the definition, the Lie algebra of a closed subgroup $H$ of $GL(n, \mathbb{R})$ is the same as the Lie algebra of the identity component of $H$.

Theorem 1.3.11

Let $H$ be a closed subgroup of $GL(n, \mathbb{R})$. There exist an open neighborhood $V$ of $0$ in $\operatorname{Lie}(H)$ and an open neighborhood $\Omega$ of $I$ in $GL(n,\mathbb{R})$ such that $\exp : \operatorname{Lie}(H) \rightarrow H$ restricts to a homeomorphism $V \rightarrow \Omega \cap H$.

A topological group $G$ is a Lie group if there is a compatible differentiable manifold structure on $G$.

Theorem 1.3.12

Let $H$ be a closed subgroup of $GL(n,\mathbb{R})$, considered as a topological group with the relative topology. Then $H$ has a Lie group structure that is compatible with its topological group structure.

1.3.6 Differentials of Homomorphism

Let $G \subset GL(n,\mathbb{R})$ and $H \subset GL(m,\mathbb{R})$ be closed subgroups.

Proposition 1.3.14

Let $\varphi : H \rightarrow G$ be a continuous homomorphism. There exists a unique Lie algebra homomorphism $d\varphi : \operatorname{Lie}(H) \rightarrow \operatorname{Lie}(G)$, called the differential of $\varphi$, such that \[ \varphi(\exp(X)) = \exp(d\varphi(X)) \ \text{ for all } X \in \operatorname{Lie}(H). \]
