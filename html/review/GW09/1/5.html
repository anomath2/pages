1.5. Rational Representations

Determining all actions of a classical group $G$ might seem much harder than studying the group directly, but it turns out, thanks to the work of \'{E}. Cartan and H. Weyl, that these representations have a very explicit structure that also yields information about $G$.

1.5.1. Definitions and Examples

Let $G$ be a linear algebraic group. A representation $(\rho, V)$ is said to be regular or rational if \[ \dim V < \infty \]and the matrix coefficients \[ \langle \phi, \rho(g) v \rangle, \phi \in V^*, v \in V \]are regular.
A representation $(\rho, V)$ is said to be locally regular if every finite-dimensional subspace of $V$ is contained in a regular subrepresentation of $V$.

Define the left and right translations on $\mathcal{O}[G]$ by \[ L(g)f(h) = f(g^{-1} h), \quad R(g)f(h) = f(hg). \]

Proposition 1.5.1

The representations $(L, \mathcal{O}[G])$ and $(R, \mathcal{O}[G])$ are locally regular.
