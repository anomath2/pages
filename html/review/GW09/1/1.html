1.1. The Classical Groups

Abstract

The classical groups are the groups of invertible linear transformations of finite-dimensional vector spaces over the real, complex, and quaternion fields, together with the subgroups that preserve a volume form, a bilinear form, or a sesquilinear form (the forms being nondegenerate and symmetric or skew-symmetric).

1.1.1. General and Special Linear Groups

Let $\mathbb{F}$ denote either the field $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, and let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$. The set of invertible linear transformations from $V$ to $V$ will be denoted by $GL(V)$.
Let $V$ and $W$ be finite-dimensional vector spaces over $\mathbb{F}$. Let $\{ v_1,\dots, v_n \}$ and $\{ w_1,\dots, w_m \}$ be bases for $V$ and $W$, respectively. If $T : V \rightarrow W$ is a linear map, then \[ T v_j = \sum_{i=1}^m a_{i,j} w_i \]with $a_{i,j} \in \mathbb{F}$. The numbers $a_{i,j}$ are called the matrix coefficients or entries of $T$ with respect to the two bases, and the matrix \[ A = (a_{i,j})_{1 \leq i \leq m, 1 \leq j \leq n} \]is called the matrix of $T$ with respect to the two bases.

Let $GL(n, \mathbb{F})$ denote the set of $n \times n$ invertible matrices with coefficients in $\mathbb{F}$. It is called the general linear group of rank $n$.
If $V$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space, then there exists a group isomorphism $GL(V) \rightarrow GL(n, \mathbb{F})$ for each basis of $V$.

Special Linear Group

The special linear group $SL(n, \mathbb{F})$ is the set of all elements $A \in GL(n, \mathbb{F})$ such that $\det(A) = 1$.
If $V$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space, and $\mu: GL(V) \rightarrow GL(n, \mathbb{F})$ the group isomorphism associated with a basis of $V$. Then, $SL(V) := \mu^{-1}(SL(n, \mathbb{F}))$ is independent of the basis.

1.1.2. Isometry Groups of Bilinear Forms

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$, and $B : V \times V \rightarrow \mathbb{F}$ a bilinear form. Let $O(V, B)$ or $O(B)$ denote the set of all $g \in GL(V)$ such that \[ B(gv, gw) = B(v,w) \text{ for all } v,w \in V. \]It is called the isometry group of the form $B$.
Let $\{ v_1,\dots,v_n \}$ be a basis of $V$, and set \[ \Gamma_{i,j} := B(v_i, v_j). \]If $A = (a_{i,j})_{i,j}$ be the matrix of an element $g$ of $GL(V)$. Then, we have $g \in O(B)$ if and only if \[ {}^t A \Gamma A = \Gamma, \]where $\Gamma := (\Gamma_{i,j})_{i,j}$.

Orthogonal Groups

Set \[ O(n, \mathbb{F}) := \{ g \in GL(n, \mathbb{F}) \mid g \cdot {}^t g = I \}. \]It is called the orthogonal group.
When $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{R}$, for $p,q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ with $p + q = n$, define the indefinite orthogonal groups by \[ O(p,q) := \{ g \in GL(n, \mathbb{R}) \mid {}^t g I_{p,q} g = I_{p,q} \}, \quad I_{p,q} := \begin{pmatrix} I_p & O \\ O & -I_q \end{pmatrix}. \]
Note that \[ O(n,0) = O(0,n) = O(n, \mathbb{R}). \]
Set \[ s := \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \end{pmatrix}. \]Then, \[ O(p,q) \rightarrow O(q,p); g \mapsto sgs \]is a group isomorphism.

Lemma 1.1.2

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$, and $B$ a symmetric nondegenerate bilinear form on $V$.
  • 1. If $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{C}$, then there exists a basis $\{ v_1,\dots,v_n \}$ of $V$ such that $B(v_i, v_j) = \delta_{i,j}$.
  • 2. If $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{R}$, then there exist $p,q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ with $p+q = n$, and a basis $\{ v_1,\dots,v_n \}$ of $V$ such that $B(v_i, v_j) = \epsilon_i \delta_{i,j}$, where \[ \epsilon_i := \begin{cases} 1 & \text{ if } 1 \leq i \leq p, \\ -1 & \text{ if } p < i \leq n. \end{cases} \] Furthermore, the pair $(p,q)$ is independent of the basis.

Remark 1.1.3

The basis of $V$ in Lemma 1.1.2 (2) is called a pseudo-orthonormal basis, and the number $p-q$ is called the signature of $B$.

Proposition 1.1.4

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$, and $B$ a symmetric nondegenerate bilinear form on $V$.
  • 1. If $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{C}$, then $O(V,B) \simeq O(n, \mathbb{C})$.
  • 2. If $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{R}$, then there exist unique $p,q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ with $p+q = n$ such that $O(V, B) \simeq O(p,q)$.

The special orthogonal group is the subgroup \[ SO(n, \mathbb{F}) := SL(n, \mathbb{F}) \cap O(n, \mathbb{F}). \]The indefinite special orthogonal groups are the subgroups \[ SO(p,q) := SL(p+q, \mathbb{R}) \cap O(p,q). \]

Symplectic Group

For $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0}$, set \[ J = J_n := \begin{pmatrix} O & I_n \\ -I_n & O \end{pmatrix}. \]The symplectic group is defined to be \[ Sp(n,\mathbb{F}) := \{ g \in GL(2n, \mathbb{F}) \mid {}^t g J g = J \}. \]

Lemma 1.1.5

Let $V$ be a $2n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$, and $B$ a nondegenerate skew-symmetric bilinear form on $V$. Then, there exists a basis $\{ v_1,\dots,v_n \}$ of $V$ such that $B(v_i,v_j)$ equals the $(i,j)$-th entry of $J$.

Proposition 1.1.6

Let $V$ be a $2n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$, and $B$ a skew-symmetric nondegenerate bilinear form on $V$. Then, $O(V,B) \simeq Sp(n, \mathbb{F})$.

1.1.3. Unitary Groups

The unitary group of rank $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0}$ is the group \[ U(n) := \{ g \in GL(n, \mathbb{C}) \mid gg^* = I \}. \]The special unitary group is \[ SU(n) := SL(n, \mathbb{C}) \cap U(n). \]The indefinite unitary group of signature $(p,q)$ is \[ U(p,q) := \{ g \in GL(n, \mathbb{C}) \mid g^* I_{p,q} g = I_{p,q} \}. \]The special indefinite unitary group of signature $(p,q)$ is \[ SU(p,q) := SL(n, \mathbb{C}) \cap U(p,q). \]

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional $\mathbb{C}$-vector space, and $B$ a Hermitian form on $V$. Set \[ U(V, B) := \{ g \in GL(V) \mid B(gv,gw) = B(v,w) \text{ for all } v,w \in V \}. \]We call it the unitary group of $B$.

Lemma 1.1.7

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ and let $B$ a nondegenerate Hermitian form on $V$. Then there exist integers $p,q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, with $p+q = n$, and a basis $\{ v_1,\dots,v_n \}$ of $V$ such that $B(v_i , v_j) = \epsilon_i \delta_{i,j}$, where \[ \epsilon_i := \begin{cases} 1 & \text{ if } 1 \leq i \leq p, \\ -1 & \text{ if } p < i \leq n. \end{cases} \]Furthermore, the pair $(p,q)$ is independent of the basis.

Proposition 1.1.8

Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ and let $B$ a nondegenerate Hermitian form on $V$. Then, there exist unique $p,q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ with $p+q = n$ such that $U(V,B) \simeq U(p,q)$.

Quaternionic Groups

Let $GL(n, \mathbb{H})$ denote the group of invertible $n \times n$ matrices over $\mathbb{H}$.

Quaternionic Special Linear Group

Set \[ SU^*(2n) := \{ g \in GL(n, \mathbb{H}) \mid det(g) = 1 \}. \]

The Quaternionic Unitary Groups

Let $p+q = n$. The indefinite quaternionic unitary groups are \[ Sp(p,q) := \{ g \in GL(n, \mathbb{H}) \mid g^* I_{p,q} g = I_{p,q} \}. \]

The Group $SO^*(2n)$

Set \[ SO^*(2n) := \{ g \in SO(2n, \mathbb{C}) \mid ^J \overline{g} J = g \}. \]
