1.1. Lie superalgebras: Definitions and examples

Throughout this book we will work over the field $\mathbb{C}$ of complex numbers. Let \[ \mathbb{Z}_2 = \{ \overline{0}, \overline{1 }\} \]denote the group of two elements and let $\mathfrak{S}_n$ denote the symmetric group in $n$ letters.

1.1.1. Basic definitions

A vector superspace $V$ is a vector space endowed with a $\mathbb{Z}_2$-gradation: \[ V = V_{\overline{0}} \oplus V_{\overline{1}}. \]The dimension of the vector superspace $V$ is the tuple \[ \dim V = (\dim V_{\overline{0}} | V_{\overline{1}}) \]or sometimes \[ \dim V = \dim V_{\overline{0}} + \dim V_{\overline{1}} \](which should be clear from the context). The superdimension of $V$ is defined to be \[ \operatorname{sdim} V := \dim V_{\overline{0}} - \dim V_{\overline{1}}. \]We denote the superspace with even subspace $\mathbb{C}^m$ and odd subspace $\mathcal{C}^n$ by $\mathbb{C}^{m|n}$. It has dimension $(m|n)$. The parity of a homogeneous element $a \in V_i$ is denoted by \[ |a| = i \in \mathbb{Z}_2. \]An element in $V_{\overline{0}}$ is called even, while an element in $V_{\overline{0}}$ is called odd. A subspace of a vector superspace $V$ is a vector superspace $W = W_{\overline{0}} \oplus W_{\overline{1}}$ with compatible $\mathbb{Z}_2$-gradation, i.e., \[ W_i \subseteq V_i \text{ for all } i \in \mathbb{Z}_2. \]
There is a parity reversing functor $\Pi$ on the category of vector superspaces. For a vector superspace $V$, we let \[ \Pi(V) = \Pi(V)_{\overline{0}} \oplus \Pi(V)_{\overline{1}}, \quad \Pi(V)_i = V_{i + \overline{1}} \text{ for all } i \in \mathbb{Z}_2. \]

Definition 1.1

A superalgebra $A$, is a vector superspace equipped with a bilinear multiplication satisfying \[ A_i A_j \subseteq A_{i+j} \text{ for all } i,j \in \mathbb{Z}_2. \]A module $M$ over a superalgebra $A$ is always understood in the $\mathbb{Z}_2$-graded sense, that is \[ A_i M_j \subseteq M_{i+j} \text{ for all } i,j \in \mathbb{Z}_2. \]Subalgebras and ideals of superalgebras are also understood in the $\mathbb{Z}_2$-graded sense. A superalgebra that has no nontrivial ideal is called simple.

Definition 1.2

A Lie superalgebra is a superalgebra $\mathfrak{g}$ with bilinear multiplication $[,]$ satisfying the following two axioms: for homogeneous elements $a,b, c \in \mathfrak{g}$,
  • (1) Skew-supersymmetry: $[a,b] = -(-1)^{|a| |b|}[b, a]$.
  • (2) Super Jacobi identity: $[a,[b,c]] = [[a,b],c] + (-1)^{|a||b|}[b,[a,c]]$.
A bilinear form $(,)$ on a Lie superalgebra $\mathfrak{g}$ is called invariant if \[ ([a,b],c) = (a,[b,c]) \text{ for all } a,b,c \in \mathfrak{g}. \]

For a Lie superalgebra $\mathfrak{g}$, the even part $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}}$ is a Lie algebra. Hence, if $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}$, then $\mathfrak{g}$ is just a usual Lie algebra.
A Lie superalgebra $\mathfrak{g}$ with purely odd part, i.e., $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}} = 0$, has to be abelian, i.e., $[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}] = 0$.

Definition 1.3

Let $\mathfrak{g}$ and $\mathfrak{g}'$ be Lie superalgebras. A homomorphism of Lie superalgebras is an even linear map $f : \mathfrak{g} \rightarrow \mathfrak{g}'$ satisfying \[ f([a,b]) = [f(a), f(b)] \text{ for all } a,b \in \mathfrak{g}. \]

Example 1.4

  • (1) Let $A$ be an associative superalgebra. We can make $A$ into a Lie superalgebra by letting \[ [a,b] := ab-(-1)^{|a||b|} ba \] for homogeneous $a,b \in A$ and extending $[,]$ by bilinearity.
  • (2) Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a Lie superalgebra. Then $\operatorname{End}(\mathfrak{g})$ is an associative superalgebra, and hence it carries a structure of a Lie superalgebra by (1). We define the adjoint map \[ \operatorname{ad}: \mathfrak{g} \rightarrow \operatorname{End}(\mathfrak{g}) \] by \[ \operatorname{ad}(a)(b) := [a,b] \text{ for all } a,b \in \mathfrak{g}. \] Then $\operatorname{ad}$ is a homomorphism of Lie superalgebras due to the super Jacobi identity. The resulting action of $\mathfrak{g}$ on itself is called the adjoint action.
  • (3) Let $A$ be a superalgebra. An endomorphism $D \in \operatorname{End}(A)_s$, for $s \in \mathbb{Z}_2$, is called a derivation of degree $s$ if it satisfies that \[ D(ab) = D(a)b + (-1)^{s |a|} a D(b) \text{ for all } a,b \in A. \] Denote by $\operatorname{Der}(A)_s$ the space of derivations on $A$ of degree $s$. One verifies that the superspace of derivations of $A$, $\operatorname{Der}(A) := \operatorname{Der}(A)_{\overline{0}} \oplus \operatorname{Der}(A)_{\overline{1}}$, is a subalgebra of the Lie superalgebra $\operatorname{End}(A)$. In the case when $\mathfrak{g}$ is a Lie superalgebra, we have \[ \operatorname{ad}(g) \in \operatorname{Der}(\mathfrak{g}) \text{ for all } g \in \mathfrak{g}, \] by the super Jacobi identity. Indeed, such derivations are called inner derivations. The inner derivations form an ideal in $\operatorname{Der}(\mathfrak{g})$.
  • (4) Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a superspace such that $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}} = \mathbb{C} z$ is one-dimensional. Suppose that we have a symmetric bilinear form $B(,)$ on $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}$. We can make $\mathfrak{g}$ into a Lie superalgebra by letting $z$ commute with $\mathfrak{g}$ and declaring \[ [v,w] := B(v,w)z \text{ for all } v,w \in \mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}. \] The special cases when $B(,)$ is zero and when $B(,)$ is non-degenerate, respectively, are of particular interest. Indeed, their corresponding universal enveloping superalgebras (see Section 1.5.1) are isomorphic to the exterior and Clifford superalgebras of Section 1.1.6 and Definition 3.33, respectively.

Remark 1.5

To a Lie superalgebra $\mathfrak{g}$ we associate the following data:
  • (1) A Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}}$.
  • (2) A $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}}$-module $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}$ induced by the adjoint action.
  • (3) A $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}}$-homomorphism $S^2(\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}) \rightarrow \mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}}$ induced by the Lie bracket.
  • (4) The condition coming from Definition 1.2 (2) with $a,b,c \in \mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}$.
Conversely, the above data determine a Lie superalgebra structure on $\mathfrak{g}_{\overline{0}} \oplus \mathfrak{g}_{\overline{1}}$.

1.1.2. The general and special linear Lie superalgebras

Let $V$ be a vector superspace so that $\operatorname{End}(V)$ is an associative superalgebra. As in Example 1.4(1), $\operatorname{End}(V)$, equipped with the supercommutator, forms a Lie superalgebra called the general linear Lie superalgebra and denoted by $\mathfrak{gl}(V)$. When $V = \mathbb{C}^{m|n}$, we also write $\mathfrak{gl}(m|n)$ for $\mathfrak{gl}(V)$.
Set \[ I(m|n) := \{ \overline{1}, \dots, \overline{m}; 1, \dots, n \} \]with total order \[ \overline{1} < \cdots < \overline{m} < 0 < 1 < \cdots < n. \]Here $0$ is inserted for notational convenience later on. Then, $\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ can be realized as $(m+n) \times (m+n)$ complex matrices of the block form \[ g = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \]such that $\mathfrak{gl}(V)_{\overline{0}}$ consists of those with $b = c = 0$, while $\mathfrak{gl}(V)_{\overline{1}}$ with $a = d = 0$.

Remark 1.6

We have an isomorphism of Lie superalgebras from $\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ to $\mathfrak{gl}(\Pi V)$ by sending $T$ to $\Pi T \Pi^{-1}$. When $\dim V = (m|n)$, we obtain an isomorphism of Lie superalgebras \[ \mathfrak{gl}(m|n) \simeq \mathfrak{gl}(n|m). \]

For each element $g \in \mathfrak{gl}(m|n)$ of the matrix form, we define the supertrace as \[ \operatorname{str}(g) := \operatorname{tr}(a) - \operatorname{tr}(d). \]The subspace \[ \mathfrak{sl}(m|n) := \{ g \in \mathfrak{gl}(m|n) \mid \operatorname{str}(g) = 0 \} \]is a subalgebra of $\mathfrak{gl}(m|n)$, and it is called the special linear Lie superalgebra. One verifies directly that \[ [\mathfrak{gl}(m|n), \mathfrak{gl}(m|n)] = \mathfrak{sl}(m|n). \]

For $A = \sum_{i,j} a_{i,j} E_{i,j} \in \mathfrak{gl}(m|n)$, define its supertranspose $A^{\mathrm{st}}$ by \[ A^{\mathrm{st}} := \sum_{i,j} (-1)^{|j|(|i|+|j|)} a_{i,j} E_{j,i}. \]
The Chevalley involution $\tau: \mathfrak{gl}(V) \rightarrow \mathfrak{gl}(V)$ is the Lie superalgebra automorphism given by \[ \tau(A) = -A^{\mathrm{st}} \]
