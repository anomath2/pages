4.1. The Hopf Algebra $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$

4.1.1. The Bialgebra $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$

Let $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$ be the complex algebra with generators $a,b,c,d$ satisfying the following relations: \begin{align*} \begin{split} &ab = qba, \ ac = qca,\ bd = qdb,\ cd = qdc, \\ &bc = cb, \\ &ad - da = (q-q^{-1})bc. \end{split} \end{align*}
Sometimes, we write \[ u_1^1 = a,\ u_2^1 = b,\ u_1^2 = c,\ u_2^2 = d. \]

Proposition 1

There exists a unique bialgebra structure on $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$ with \begin{align*} \begin{split} &\Delta(a) = a \otimes a + b \otimes c, \ \Delta(b) = a \otimes b + b \otimes d, \\ &\Delta(c) = c \otimes a + d \otimes c, \ \Delta(d) = c \otimes b + d \otimes d, \\ &\epsilon(a) = \epsilon(d) = 1,\ \epsilon(b) = \epsilon(c) = 0. \end{split} \end{align*}
In other words, \[ \Delta(u^i_j) = \sum_k u^i_k \otimes u^k_j, \ \epsilon(u^i_j) = \delta_{i,j}. \]

Definition 1

The bialgebra $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$ is called the coordinate algebra of the quantum matrix space $M_q(2)$.

We give a reformulation of the six quadratic relations for $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$. Define the matrix $R = (R^{i,j}_{k,l})$ by \[ R^{11}_{11} = R^{22}_{22} = q,\ R^{12}_{12} = R^{21}_{21} = 1, \ R^{21}_{12} = q-q^{-1}, \]and $R^{ij}_{kl} = 0$ otherwise.
Consider the following 16 equations: \[ \sum_{k,l} R^{i,j}_{k,l} u^k_n u^l_m = \sum_{k,l} u^j_l u^i_k R^{k,l}_{n,m}, \quad i,j,n,m = 1,2. \]These reduce precisely to the 6 relations above. This formulation is the starting point for the FRT approach to quantum groups.

4.1.2. The Hopf Algebra $\mathcal{O}(SL_2(2))$

The quantum determinant $\mathcal{D}_q$ is \[ \mathcal{D}_q := ad-qbc = da-q^{-1}bc \in \mathcal{O}(M_q(2)). \]

Proposition 2

The $\mathcal{D}_q$ is a group-like element, i.e., \[ \Delta(\mathcal{D}_q) = \mathcal{D}_q \otimes \mathcal{D}_q, \ \epsilon(\mathcal{D}_q) = 1, \]and belongs to the center of $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$.

Since $\mathcal{D}_q$ is group-like, the two-sided ideal of $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$ generated by $\mathcal{D}_q-1$ is a biideal. Hence, \[ \mathcal{O}(SL_q(2)) := \mathcal{O}(M_q(2))/(D_q-1) \]is a bialgebra.

Proposition 3

The bialgebra $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$ is a Hopf algebra. The antipode $S$ is given by \[ S(a) = d,\ S(b) = -q^{-1}b,\ S(c) = -qc,\ S(d) = a. \]

Definition 2

The Hopf algebra $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$ is called the coordinate algebra of the quantum group $SL_q(2)$

Proposition 4

The set \[ \{ a^n b^m c^r,\ b^m c^r d^s \mid m,r,s \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}, n \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0} \} \]is a basis of $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$.

4.1.3. A Geometric Approach to $SL_q(2)$

We shall see that the defining relations of $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$ occur quite naturally if we think of quantum matrices of $M_q(2)$ as transformations of the quantum plane $\mathbb{C}_q^2$.
Let $\mathcal{O}(\mathbb{C}_q^2)$ be the algebra with generators $x,y$ satisfying the relation \[ xy = qyx. \]We call $\mathcal{O}(\mathbb{C}_q^2)$ the coordinate algebra of the quantum plane $\mathbb{C}_q^2$

Proposition 7

The $\mathcal{O}(\mathbb{C}_q^2)$ is a left and right comodule algebra of the bialgebra $\mathcal{O}(M_q(2))$ with left coaction $\varphi_L$ and right coaction $\varphi_R$ given by \begin{align*} \begin{split} &\varphi_L(x) = a \otimes x + b \otimes y, \quad \varphi_L(y) = c \otimes x + d \otimes y, \\ &\varphi_R(x) = x \otimes a + y \otimes c, \quad \varphi_R(y) = x \otimes b + y \otimes d. \end{split} \end{align*}

The assertions of Proposition 7 remain valid for $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$.

The exterior algebra $\Lambda(\mathbb{C}_q^2)$ of the quantum plane $\mathbb{C}_q^2$ is the algebra generated by $\xi, \eta$ subject to the following relations: \[ \xi^2 = \eta^2 = 0, \quad \xi \eta = -q^{-1} \eta \xi. \]
Setting $\xi' := a \otimes \xi + b \otimes \eta$ and $\eta' := c \otimes \xi + d \otimes \eta$, we have \[ \xi' \eta' = (ad-qbc) \otimes \xi\eta. \]Hence, it is natural to take $ad-qbc$ as the quantum determinant.

4.1.4. Real Forms of $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$

We list 3 real forms of $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2))$.

Case 1

Suppose that $q \in \mathbb{R}$, and set \[ a^* = d, \ b^* = -qc,\ c^* = -q^{-1}b,\ d^* = a. \]The corresponding Hopf $*$-algebra is called the coordinate algebra of the real quantum group $SU_q(2)$, and is denoted by $\mathcal{O}(SU_q(2))$.

Set \[ a^* = d,\ b^* = qc,\ c^* = q^{-1}b,\ d^* = a. \]The corresponding Hopf $*$-algebra is called the coordinate algebra of the real quantum group $SU_q(1,1)$, and is denoted by $\mathcal{O}(SU_q(1,1))$.

Case 2

Suppose that $|q| = 1$, and set \[ a^* = a,\ b^* = b,\ c^* = c,\ d^* = d. \]The corresponding Hopf $*$-algebra is denoted by $\mathcal{O}(SL_q(2,\mathbb{R}))$.
