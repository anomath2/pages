1. Classical Compact Symmetric Spaces

The material treated in this section is well known by now [HS94], [H00], [H01].
Let $G$ be a compact connected simple Lie group. To simplify our statements we assume that $G$ is simply connected, but this is not essential. Let $K \subset G$ be a closed subgroup such that there exists a (necessarily unique) involutive automorphism $\theta$ on $G$ with $G^\theta_0 \subset K \subset G^\theta$. Here $G^\theta$ denotes the subgroup of fixed points of $\theta$, $G^\theta_0$ its connected component of the identity. Then the pair $(G, K)$ is an irreducible Riemannian symmetric pair, and the $G$-homogeneous space $G/K$ is an irreducible Riemannian symmetric space of compact type I (in the terminology of Helgason [H01]).

The pair $(G, K)$ is a Gelfand pair, i.e., for each finite-dimensional irreducible representation $\pi$ of $G$, the trivial representation of $K$ occurs at most once in the irreducible decomposition of $\pi$ as a representation of $K$. Those irreducible representations of G that have nonzero $K$-fixed vectors are called spherical representations.

Let $\mathcal{D}(G/K)$ denote the algebra of left $G$-invariant differential operators on the space $G/K$. A (zonal) spherical function is by definition a $K$-bi-invariant differentiable function on $G$ which is a joint eigenfunction of all differential operators $D \in \mathcal{D}(G/K)$.

Let $P^+$ denote the set of dominant weights for $G$. Let $P^+_K \subset P^+$ denote the subset consisting of $\lambda \in P^+$ such that the irreducible representation $G(\lambda)$ is spherical. If we write $\mathcal{H}$ for the algebra of $K$-bi-invariant (continuous) representative functions on $G$, then there is the canonical decomposition \[ \mathcal{H} = \bigoplus_{\lambda \in P^+_K} \mathcal{H}(\lambda), \]where $\mathcal{H}(\lambda)$ is the intersection of $\mathcal{H}$ and the subspace $W(\lambda) \subset L^2(G)$ spanned by the matrix coefficients of the representation $V(\lambda)$. Each $\mathcal{H}(\lambda)$ is one-dimensional. A nonzero $\varphi_\lambda \in \mathcal{H}(\lambda)$ is a spherical function, and all spherical functions are obtained in this way.
